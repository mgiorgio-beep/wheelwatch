Crews
Fish with your crew, not the internet
The most-requested feature is here. Private groups for captains who trust each other. You control what you share, who sees it, and when.
Starting a crew
New

Go to the Crews tab and tap "+ New". Give your crew a name — "Chatham Fleet", "The Usual Suspects", whatever works. Wheelhouse generates a short invite code on the spot. Share that code however you normally talk to your captains — text, radio, dock. Anyone who enters it joins immediately. You're the captain of the crew, which means you can see who's in, remove members if needed, and transfer the role to someone else if you want to step back.

Takes about 30 seconds to create — name it, get the code, share it
No limit on crew size — two captains or twenty, same setup
You can create multiple crews — a tight inner circle and a broader regional group, for example
As captain you can remove members at any time and transfer the captain role if needed
How to start a crew
1
Open Wheelhouse and tap the Crews tab at the bottom of the screen
2
Tap + New in the top right and enter a name for your crew
3
Your invite code appears instantly — text it to whoever you want in the crew
4
Turn on catch sharing for that crew and the feed goes live — you'll see their catches, they'll see yours
Joining a crew
New

If someone sends you an invite code, go to the Crews tab, enter the code in the Join field, and tap Join. You're in immediately — no waiting, no approval needed. You can be in as many crews as you want at the same time. A tight two-man, the full Chatham fleet, a regional group — each one is completely separate with its own feed and sharing settings.

Invite code based — nobody gets in without a code from the captain
No limit on how many crews you can join
Joining a crew doesn't automatically turn on sharing — you control that separately
You can leave a crew at any time from the crew settings — no notification goes to the captain

Sharing controls
New

Each crew has its own sharing toggle. You decide per crew whether your catches are visible to that group. You could be in three crews, share with two, and stay dark in one. Nobody knows you're there unless you're sharing.

Per-crew toggle — sharing is not all-or-nothing
Turn sharing on or off at any time, mid-season, mid-trip
GPS coordinates are never shared with anyone under any circumstance — that's a hard technical restriction, not a setting

Feeds are per-crew — your Chatham Fleet feed is separate from your other crews

Predictive Engine v2
Rebuilt

Before, the advisor worked off live conditions and its own knowledge of Monomoy. Now it matches today's exact setup against three years of historical conditions and actual logged catches — and tells you what's been producing under setups like today's.

Tide-relative matching — Not what time it is. Where you are in the cycle. A 2-hour flood fishes the same at 7 AM or 1 PM.
SST gradient — Sound side vs Atlantic side temp break, and whether it's strengthening or weakening. Bait stacks on the break edge.
Solunar periods — Major and minor feeding windows driven by the moon's gravitational pull on water. Same physics as the tide table. When a major period stacks on a moving flood at a rip, that's your window.
Water temp, wind, chlorophyll — Stripers have a preferred range. Chlorophyll shows where bait is concentrated. Wind affects sea state and feeding.
Spring vs neap awareness — Spring tides produce stronger rips. The engine knows the difference and weights it.
Example — same question, two engines
Question
"Leaving Ryder's Cove at 6 AM tomorrow. Striper fishing. What's the plan?"
Before
Tide is rising at 6 AM, high at 10:42. Water temp around 54°F, SW wind 12 knots. Conditions look reasonable for stripers. Try the rips on the incoming. Stonehorse and Pollock Rip are typical spots this time of year.
Now
Strong setup. Major solunar hits at 6:40 AM — right as you're clearing the cuts. You'll be 3.5 hours into the flood at departure, peak flow at Pollock Rip around 8:15.

SST break is running 8°F between the Sound and Atlantic sides — sharpest it's been in 10 days and still strengthening. That edge has been stacking bait.

Conditions today are consistent with setups that have historically produced at Monomoy. Casting has been the stronger technique under similar tide and temperature conditions. Spring tide, so the rip will be running hard — give Stonehorse 6 AM to 9 AM before it peaks and flattens out.

On the horizon
What's coming next
Wheelhouse is built around Monomoy and the Chatham fleet today. The plan is to expand — more water, more captains, sharper predictions everywhere.
Expanded coverage area
Coming

The current data grid is centered on Monomoy Shoals. The plan is to extend SST monitoring points, tide stations, current data, and satellite coverage across the full Cape and Islands region — giving every captain in the fleet real-time intelligence for wherever they're fishing.

Nauset to Provincetown — Race Point, Peaked Hill, Nauset Inlet, outer beach rips from Orleans to Truro
South to south of Nantucket — Great Point, Sankaty Head, Cross Rip, Davis South Shoal, and the offshore temperature breaks beyond the shelf edge
Nantucket Sound — Horseshoe Shoal, Handkerchief, Bass River, south side of Hyannis out to Vineyard Sound
Vineyard Sound — Woods Hole, Quick's Hole, Devil's Bridge, Squibnocket Point
Each area gets its own SST monitoring points, tide stations, chlorophyll readings, and historical data seeding — same intelligence Chatham has today
Per-area pattern engines
Coming

As the fleet grows beyond Chatham, the predictive engine will build separate pattern databases for each region. Catches logged at Race Point train the Race Point engine. Catches at Great Point train that one. The intelligence stays local and accurate — not averaged across 100 miles of coastline.

Each area accumulates its own historical conditions and catch patterns independently
Captains who fish multiple areas contribute to all of them automatically based on their logged catch locations
Species beyond striped bass
Coming

The current engine is tuned for stripers. As the dataset grows the plan is to add dedicated pattern models for false albacore, bluefish, and bluefin — each with their own seasonal baselines, preferred conditions, and historical data. Albies in particular have very specific thermal and current preferences that the SST gradient data is already perfectly positioned to track.

False albacore — SST and chlorophyll driven, typically following the 68–72°F band
Bluefish — less temperature-specific, more tide and bait-stack driven
Bluefin — offshore SST breaks, chlorophyll edges, and current seams south of Nantucket
